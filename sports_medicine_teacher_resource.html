Standard 1 · FUND
Fundamentals
6% of exam

Key Concepts

  • 01Primary sports medicine team members: Coach, Athlete, Parents, Team Physician, Certified Athletic Trainer (AT), Allied Health Professionals
  • 02Negligence requires 4 elements: Duty of care, Breach of duty, Damage/injury occurred, Proximate cause
  • 03HIPAA protects athlete Protected Health Information (PHI) — cannot share without consent
  • 04Preparticipation Physical Examination (PPE) identifies conditions that may disqualify or predispose to injury
  • 05Assumption of Risk: athletes accept inherent dangers of sport — does NOT cover staff negligence
  • 06Informed consent: patient understands risks, benefits, alternatives BEFORE agreeing to treatment
  • 07Good Samaritan Law: protects good-faith emergency responders from liability
  • 08Title IX (1972): prohibits sex discrimination in federally funded educational programs
  • 09Scope of practice: what an AT is legally permitted to do — defined by state practice acts
  • 10Emergency Action Plan (EAP): contacts, AED location, 911 protocol, roles, evacuation routes
  • 11Risk classification: Collision > Contact > Limited Contact > Non-contact
  • 12Standard of care: level of care a reasonable professional would provide in same circumstances
⚡ Exam Tip
Know all 4 elements of negligence and be able to distinguish between the different legal terms. The AT's scope of practice is a frequent test question.

Sample Practice Questions

Q1
Which of the following is considered a PRIMARY member of the sports medicine team?
  1. ACertified Athletic Trainer (AT)✓
  2. BSchool principal
  3. CTeam sponsor
  4. DInsurance adjuster
Rationale: Primary members: Coach, Athlete, Parents, Team Physician, Certified Athletic Trainer (AT), and Allied Health Professionals. AT is the core clinical professional on the team.
Q2
'Assumption of Risk' in sports law means:
  1. AAthletes voluntarily accept the inherent risks associated with participating in a sport✓
  2. BThe school assumes all financial liability for athlete injuries
  3. CAthletes waive their right to any medical treatment
  4. DCoaches assume responsibility for all player decisions
Rationale: Assumption of Risk: participants accept inherent dangers of a sport. Does NOT cover negligence by staff. Example: a football player assumes risk of contact, not risk of an untreated injury.
Q3
The Good Samaritan Law protects:
  1. AIndividuals who provide reasonable emergency assistance in good faith from liability✓
  2. BAthletic trainers only when performing surgical procedures
  3. CTeam physicians from malpractice claims during games
  4. DCoaches who provide first aid without formal training
Rationale: Good Samaritan laws protect bystanders who assist in emergencies in good faith and without gross negligence. Encourages people to help without fear of liability. Varies by state.

Standard 2 · PSYC
Sports Psychology
4% of exam

Key Concepts

  • 015 psychological phases after injury (DABDA): Denial → Anger → Bargaining → Depression → Acceptance
  • 02SMART goals: Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound
  • 03Intrinsic motivation: internal drive (fun, mastery) vs Extrinsic: external rewards (trophies, praise)
  • 04Burnout vs Staleness: Burnout = chronic exhaustion + withdrawal; Staleness = temporary performance plateau
  • 05Progressive Muscle Relaxation (PMR): tense then release muscle groups to reduce anxiety
  • 06Visual imagery: mentally rehearsing skills to strengthen neural pathways — used in rehab AND performance
  • 07Overtraining interventions: time off, athlete autonomy, variety, reduced demands
  • 08Goal types: Process (technique) > Performance (personal PR) > Outcome (win/lose)
⚡ Exam Tip
DABDA phases appear frequently — know the correct ORDER. Distinguish burnout (chronic, serious) from staleness (temporary, fixable with rest).

Sample Practice Questions

Q1
The five psychological phases an athlete experiences following an injury, in correct order, are:
  1. ADenial → Anger → Bargaining → Depression → Acceptance✓
  2. BAnger → Denial → Acceptance → Depression → Bargaining
  3. CDepression → Denial → Bargaining → Anger → Acceptance
  4. DAcceptance → Anger → Denial → Bargaining → Depression
Rationale: Kübler-Ross grief model (DABDA) applied to athletic injury: Denial → Anger → Bargaining → Depression → Acceptance. Not always linear — athletes may cycle through stages.
Q2
Visual imagery as a psychological performance technique involves:
  1. AMentally rehearsing athletic skills and scenarios to strengthen neural pathways and improve execution✓
  2. BWatching video footage of an opponent's previous performances
  3. CUsing mirrors during practice to monitor and correct technique
  4. DVisualizing an opponent's weaknesses to create competitive advantage
Rationale: Mental imagery (visualization): creates or strengthens neurological motor patterns without physical movement. Used in rehabilitation AND performance. PETTLEP model guides effective imagery practice.
Q3
Progressive muscle relaxation (PMR) as an anxiety management technique involves:
  1. ASystematically tensing and then releasing muscle groups throughout the body to reduce physical tension and anxiety✓
  2. BRunning at progressively increasing speeds to build cardiovascular confidence
  3. CGradually adding weight to a strength training program over time
  4. DA series of dynamic warm-up movements performed before competition
Rationale: PMR: tense a muscle group maximally for 5-10 seconds → release completely → notice the contrast. Progressively move through the body. Reduces somatic anxiety and promotes body awareness.

Standard 3 · HEAL
Injury & Healing Process
12% of exam

Key Concepts

  • 015 cardinal signs of inflammation (SHARP): Swelling, Heat, Altered function, Redness, Pain
  • 023 healing phases: Acute/Inflammation (0–72hrs) → Subacute/Repair (3 days–3 weeks) → Remodeling (3 weeks–2 years)
  • 03Vasodilation in acute phase: increases blood flow, delivers WBCs, oxygen, and nutrients to injury
  • 04Hypoxia causes secondary cell death — why RICE is critical in first 72 hours
  • 05Subacute phase: fibroblasts produce collagen → scar tissue formation
  • 06Remodeling phase: collagen reorganizes along lines of mechanical stress (Wolff's Law)
  • 07Wolff's Law: bone/tissue adapts its structure to the mechanical stresses placed upon it
  • 08HIPS assessment: History → Inspection → Palpation → Special Tests
  • 09Injury grades: Grade I = microscopic/mild; Grade II = partial tear/moderate; Grade III = complete rupture
  • 10Fracture types: Greenstick (children), Comminuted (shattered), Spiral (twisting), Transverse (straight), Longitudinal
  • 11Anatomical planes: Sagittal (L/R), Frontal/Coronal (front/back), Transverse (upper/lower)
  • 12SOAP Note: Subjective → Objective → Assessment → Plan
⚡ Exam Tip
This is 12% of the exam — know it deeply. Healing phase TIMELINES are tested heavily. Wolff's Law, HIPS components, and fracture types are guaranteed questions.

Sample Practice Questions

Q1
What are the FIVE cardinal signs of the acute inflammatory response?
  1. AHeat, Redness, Swelling, Pain, Loss of Function✓
  2. BHeat, Redness, Bruising, Pain, Numbness
  3. CSwelling, Crepitus, Pain, Heat, Effusion
  4. DRedness, Pain, Atrophy, Heat, Edema
Rationale: SHARP — Swelling, Heat, Altered function, Redness, Pain — are the 5 cardinal signs of inflammation.
Q2
Hypoxia following acute injury leads to:
  1. ASecondary cell death in tissue surrounding the primary injury✓
  2. BImmediate pain relief through endorphin release
  3. CIncreased collagen production by fibroblasts
  4. DFaster removal of inflammatory mediators
Rationale: Reduced oxygen (hypoxia) in the injury zone causes secondary cell death — why ice and elevation are used to limit hypoxic spread.
Q3
Which type of fracture involves the bone shattering into three or more fragments?
  1. AComminuted✓
  2. BSpiral
  3. CTransverse
  4. DLongitudinal
Rationale: Comminuted = 'crushed' — multiple fragments. Spiral = twisting force. Transverse = straight across the shaft.

Standard 4 · HEAD
Head & Neck Injuries
8% of exam

Key Concepts

  • 01Concussion = functional brain injury (NOT structural) — LOC occurs in <10% of cases
  • 02Subdural hematoma: bleeding BELOW the dura mater (venous, slower)
  • 03Epidural hematoma: bleeding ABOVE the dura mater (arterial, rapid) — 'talk and die' = EMERGENCY
  • 04Brachial plexus originates from C5–T1 (cervical 5 through thoracic 1)
  • 05Burner/Stinger: brachial plexus stretched when neck flexes away while shoulder depressed
  • 06NOCSAE: helmets must never be modified — voids certification and creates legal liability
  • 07SCM muscle: ipsilateral lateral flexion + contralateral rotation of head
  • 08Concussion RTP protocol: stepwise, symptom-free progression — physician clearance required
  • 09Skull bones: Frontal, Occipital, Parietal, Temporal, Mandible, Maxillae, Zygomatic, Nasal
  • 10Occipital bone contains the foramen magnum — where brainstem becomes spinal cord
  • 11Referred pain: perceived at location distant from actual injury site
⚡ Exam Tip
Know subdural vs epidural — which is arterial (epidural, fast, emergency) vs venous (subdural, slower). Brachial plexus C5-T1 appears often. The 'lucid interval' = epidural hematoma.

Sample Practice Questions

Q1
A concussion is BEST defined as:
  1. AA traumatic brain injury caused by a blow or jolt to the head that alters brain function — no loss of consciousness required✓
  2. BA skull fracture with bleeding between the dura and the brain
  3. CPermanent brain damage from a single impact
  4. DLoss of consciousness lasting more than 60 seconds after head trauma
Rationale: Concussion = functional brain injury, NOT structural. Loss of consciousness occurs in <10% of concussions. Any altered mental status (confusion, amnesia, slowed processing) = concussion.
Q2
A 'burner' or 'stinger' injury in athletics is caused by:
  1. AStretching or compression of the brachial plexus from neck lateral flexion away from the shoulder being depressed✓
  2. BDirect blow to the occiput causing concussion
  3. CHyperflexion of the cervical spine causing spinal cord compression
  4. DRotation injury to the C1-C2 junction
Rationale: Stinger = neck laterally flexes AWAY while shoulder is DEPRESSED → brachial plexus is stretched. Results in burning/tingling radiating down the arm. Must be distinguished from cervical injury.
Q3
The return-to-play protocol for concussion requires:
  1. AA stepwise, symptom-free progression through activity levels before full return, cleared by a physician✓
  2. BImmediate return once headache resolves, typically within 24 hours
  3. CTwo weeks of rest regardless of symptom resolution
  4. DReturn to play when the athlete subjectively feels ready
Rationale: Return to Play (RTP) protocol: 6 steps from complete rest to full contact practice. Each step = 24 hours minimum. ANY symptom return = back to previous step. Physician clearance required.

Standard 5 · NUTR
Sports Nutrition
4% of exam

Key Concepts

  • 01Macronutrient calories: Carbohydrates = 4 kcal/g, Proteins = 4 kcal/g, Fats = 9 kcal/g
  • 02Carbohydrates = primary fuel for high-intensity exercise; stored as glycogen in muscle and liver
  • 03Hyponatremia: low blood sodium from OVERDRINKING plain water — not dehydration
  • 04Carbohydrate loading: beneficial only for endurance events >90 minutes
  • 05Pre-event meal: 3–4 hours before — high carb, low fat/fiber, moderate protein
  • 06Post-exercise window: within 30–60 minutes — 3:1 to 4:1 CHO:protein ratio
  • 07Sports drinks (6–8% CHO + electrolytes): replace fluid, electrolytes, carbs in events >60 min
  • 08Dehydration: even 2% body weight loss impairs performance significantly
  • 09Water = most critical nutrient for thermoregulation via sweating/evaporative cooling
  • 10Protein role: tissue repair, enzymes, hormones, immune function — NOT primary energy source
⚡ Exam Tip
The 3 macronutrient calorie values (4-4-9) are guaranteed questions. Hyponatremia = too MUCH water, not too little. Carb loading only helps for events >90 minutes.

Sample Practice Questions

Q1
Which macronutrient provides 4 kcal per gram AND is the body's preferred and primary fuel source during exercise?
  1. ACarbohydrates — stored as glycogen in muscle and liver✓
  2. BFats — stored as triglycerides in adipose tissue
  3. CProteins — made of amino acid chains
  4. DVitamins — required in small amounts for metabolic reactions
Rationale: Carbohydrates = 4 kcal/g. Proteins = 4 kcal/g. Fats = 9 kcal/g. Carbs are the primary fuel for high-intensity exercise. Glycogen depletion = 'hitting the wall.' Essential for sport performance.
Q2
A pre-event meal should ideally be consumed approximately:
  1. A3–4 hours before competition to allow digestion and reduce GI distress✓
  2. BImmediately before competition for maximum energy availability
  3. COnly the night before to avoid any race-day eating
  4. D45 minutes before exercise for peak blood glucose
Rationale: Pre-event meal: 3-4 hours before = optimal. Focus: high carb, moderate protein, LOW fat and fiber (slow digestion = GI distress). Small snack (30-60g carb) OK at 30-60 min if needed.
Q3
Which nutrient provides 9 kcal per gram — the highest energy density of any macronutrient?
  1. AFats (lipids) — triglycerides stored in adipose tissue✓
  2. BCarbohydrates — stored as glycogen
  3. CProteins — structural and functional molecules
  4. DAlcohol — technically provides 7 kcal/g
Rationale: Fat = 9 kcal/g. This is why fat is an efficient energy store. Primary fuel for low-intensity, long-duration exercise. Essential fatty acids (omega-3, omega-6) are required for inflammation regulation.

Standard 6 · BODY
Body Composition & Diseases
4% of exam

Key Concepts

  • 01Body composition methods: Hydrostatic (water displacement), BIA (electrical current), Skinfold calipers, Bod Pod (air displacement), DEXA (gold standard)
  • 02DEXA = gold standard: measures fat mass, lean mass, AND bone mineral density simultaneously
  • 03Hydrostatic: fat floats (less dense), muscle sinks (more dense) — Archimedes principle
  • 04BIA: fat impedes electrical current; affected by hydration status
  • 05Female Athlete Triad: Low energy availability + Menstrual dysfunction + Low bone mineral density
  • 06Anorexia Nervosa: restriction, fear of weight gain, distorted body image — highest psychiatric mortality
  • 07Bulimia Nervosa: binge → purge cycles; body weight often appears normal
  • 08Muscle Dysmorphia: obsessive belief body is too small/not muscular enough
  • 09Safe weight loss: 0.5–1.0 lb/week to preserve lean muscle mass
  • 10BMI = least accurate body composition method (doesn't distinguish muscle from fat)
⚡ Exam Tip
Know what each body composition METHOD uses (water, electricity, air, X-ray). Female Athlete Triad = 3 interconnected conditions. DEXA is the gold standard — but hydrostatic was the traditional criterion method.

Sample Practice Questions

Q1
Which body composition assessment method uses the principle of Archimedes — measuring body density through underwater weighing?
  1. AHydrostatic (underwater) weighing — the reference standard method✓
  2. BBioelectrical Impedance Analysis (BIA) — sends electrical current through tissue
  3. CSkinfold calipers — measures subcutaneous fat thickness at specific sites
  4. DBod Pod — measures air displacement in a sealed chamber
Rationale: Hydrostatic weighing: weight in water vs. on land. Fat floats (less dense), muscle sinks (more dense). Used as a criterion method. Limitations: cost, requires full submersion, breath control.
Q2
Anorexia Nervosa is characterized by:
  1. AExtreme restriction of food intake, intense fear of weight gain, distorted body image, and significantly low body weight✓
  2. BRecurrent binge eating followed by compensatory behaviors (purging, laxatives, fasting)
  3. CObsessive pursuit of muscular physique through extreme diet and exercise
  4. DDisordered eating triggered only by the pressure of athletic competition
Rationale: Anorexia: restriction, fear of fatness, distorted body image, BMI <17.5. Highest mortality rate of any psychiatric disorder. Medical complications: cardiac arrhythmias, bone loss, refeeding syndrome.
Q3
Skinfold calipers estimate body fat percentage by:
  1. AMeasuring the thickness of subcutaneous fat at standardized anatomical sites — then applying prediction equations✓
  2. BSending electrical current through the body to measure tissue resistance
  3. CMeasuring body weight before and after exercise to determine fat mass change
  4. DComparing circumference measurements to height-weight tables
Rationale: Skinfold method: measures fat under the skin at 3-7+ sites (triceps, subscapular, suprailiac, etc.). Uses Jackson-Pollock or Durnin-Womersley equations. Error: 3-4% with trained technician. Cost-effective field method.

Standard 7 · LOEX
Lower Extremity Injuries
19% of exam

Key Concepts

  • 01ACL: prevents ANTERIOR translation of tibia on femur — most common serious knee injury
  • 02Inversion ankle sprain: most commonly injures ATF (Anterior Talofibular) ligament FIRST
  • 03MCL tested with VALGUS stress (force toward midline); LCL tested with VARUS stress
  • 04Medial meniscus torn more often — attached to MCL, less mobile
  • 05Medial Tibial Stress Syndrome (shin splints): overuse, training volume spike, overpronation
  • 06Dorsiflexion = foot toward shin; Plantarflexion = pointing toes down
  • 07Eversion = sole outward; Inversion = sole inward
  • 08Plantar fasciitis: heel pain WORST with first morning steps
  • 09Turf toe: hyperextension sprain of 1st metatarsophalangeal (MTP) joint
  • 10Achilles tendon: joins gastrocnemius + soleus to calcaneus — largest tendon in body
  • 11Q-angle: larger angle (common in females) increases lateral patellar tracking forces
  • 12Prophylactic taping: Stirrups run under heel from anchor to anchor — primary structural component
  • 13Pre-wrap: protects skin only — does NOT provide support
  • 14Patella: sesamoid bone within quadriceps tendon — increases mechanical advantage
  • 15Lower extremity bones: Femur, Tibia, Fibula, Patella, Talus, Calcaneus, Metatarsals, Phalanges
⚡ Exam Tip
This is 19% of the exam — the LARGEST standard. ACL function, ATF as first ligament injured, valgus/varus stress tests, and taping terminology are tested repeatedly. Know your ankle movements cold.

Standard 8 · PERF
Performance Enhancement
8% of exam

Key Concepts

  • 01VO2max = maximum oxygen consumption during maximal exercise — gold standard of aerobic fitness
  • 02Cardiac output (CO) = Heart Rate (HR) × Stroke Volume (SV)
  • 03SPECIFICITY principle: body adapts specifically to the TYPE of stress placed on it (SAID)
  • 04Periodization: Macrocycle (year) → Mesocycles (months) → Microcycles (weeks)
  • 05Target Heart Rate: 60–85% of HRmax (HRmax = 220 − age)
  • 06Borg RPE Scale: 6 (no exertion) to 20 (maximal) — RPE × 10 ≈ heart rate
  • 07Fartlek training: unstructured spontaneous speed/intensity variation within continuous run
  • 08Aerobic: uses oxygen, 36–38 ATP/glucose, sustainable; Anaerobic: no oxygen, 2 ATP, brief, lactic acid
  • 09Long-term endurance adaptations: increased SV, decreased resting HR, increased VO2max
  • 10Conditioning principles: Adaptation, Overload, Specificity, Reversibility, Periodization
⚡ Exam Tip
CO = HR × SV is a guaranteed formula question. Know the difference between aerobic (sustainable, O2, 36-38 ATP) and anaerobic (brief, no O2, 2 ATP, lactic acid). Fartlek = speed play in Swedish.

Sample Practice Questions

Q1
VO2max is the gold standard measure of cardiovascular fitness because it measures:
  1. AMaximum oxygen consumption during maximal exercise — the ceiling of aerobic energy production✓
  2. BMaximum heart rate achievable during graded exercise testing
  3. CThe heart's maximum stroke volume at rest
  4. DBlood lactate concentration at anaerobic threshold
Rationale: VO2max = maximum rate of oxygen consumption during maximal exercise. Higher VO2max = greater aerobic capacity. Measured in mL/kg/min. Influenced by training, genetics, altitude.
Q2
The Borg Scale of Perceived Exertion (RPE) is used to:
  1. ASubjectively rate exercise intensity based on how hard the effort FEELS, correlating with heart rate✓
  2. BObjectively measure blood lactate concentration during exercise

Q3
Fartlek training is BEST described as:
  1. AUnstructured interval training that varies speed and intensity spontaneously throughout a continuous run✓
  2. BRunning at a fixed, constant pace for a predetermined distance
  3. CAlternating high-intensity intervals with complete rest periods
  4. DCircuit training combining cardiovascular and strength exercises
Rationale: Fartlek (Swedish: 'speed play') = informal, spontaneous variation of intensity within a continuous run. Example: sprint to next tree, jog to corner, stride the hill. Great for developing aerobic AND anaerobic systems.

Standard 9 · STRN
Strength, Flexibility & Ergogenic Aids
8% of exam

Key Concepts

  • 01Sliding filament theory: myosin heads pull actin toward center → sarcomere shortens → muscle contracts
  • 02Eccentric contraction: muscle LENGTHENS under load (most force, most DOMS)
  • 03Concentric contraction: muscle shortens while producing force
  • 04Type I fibers (slow-twitch): oxidative, fatigue-resistant — endurance activities
  • 05Type II fibers (fast-twitch): glycolytic, fatigue quickly — power/sprint activities
  • 06PNF stretching: contract muscle BEFORE stretching → autogenic inhibition → greater ROM
  • 07Static stretching BEST after exercise when muscles are warm (may reduce power if done before)
  • 08Plyometrics: stretch-shortening cycle — eccentric pre-load → immediate concentric release = power
  • 09Creatine: increases phosphocreatine stores → rapid ATP regeneration for 3–15 sec maximal efforts
  • 10Anabolic steroids dangers: liver damage, LVH, hormonal disruption, premature growth plate closure
  • 11EPO/Blood doping: artificially increases RBCs → more O2 delivery; risk of thrombosis, stroke, death
  • 12Ballistic stretching: bouncing at end range (activates stretch reflex — AVOID); Dynamic = controlled
⚡ Exam Tip
Sliding filament = actin slides over myosin (filaments don't shorten). PNF = contract THEN stretch. Distinguish static (after), dynamic (before), ballistic (avoid). Anabolic steroid dangers are a common test question.

Notes

Sample Practice Questions

Q1
In the sliding filament theory of muscle contraction, shortening of the sarcomere occurs when:
  1. AActin (thin filaments) slide over myosin (thick filaments) toward the center of the sarcomere✓
  2. BMyosin filaments physically shorten in length during contraction
  3. CBoth actin and myosin decrease in length simultaneously
  4. DATP breaks the actin-myosin cross-bridge preventing further contraction
Rationale: Sliding filament: myosin heads attach to actin → pull actin toward center → sarcomere shortens → MUSCLE CONTRACTS. The filaments themselves do NOT shorten — only their overlap increases.
Q2
PNF (Proprioceptive Neuromuscular Facilitation) stretching works on the principle of:
  1. AContracting the target muscle BEFORE stretching it to trigger autogenic inhibition, allowing greater flexibility gains✓
  2. BRapidly bouncing in and out of a stretch to activate muscle spindles
  3. CHolding a static stretch for 60+ seconds without any contraction
  4. DPassive stretching by a partner without any voluntary muscle activation
Rationale: PNF: Contract-relax technique — isometrically contract the muscle → relax → stretch. The contraction activates Golgi tendon organs → inhibits muscle spindles → greater relaxation = more flexibility.
Q3

  1. AAfter activity (cool-down) when muscles are warm and pliable, held 20–30 seconds per stretch✓
  2. BImmediately before high-intensity activity as the ONLY warm-up
  3. CDuring strength training sets between maximum effort exercises
  4. DDuring competition to prevent muscle cramps
Rationale: Static stretching BEFORE exercise may temporarily reduce power output (acute stretch weakness). Best AFTER exercise when muscles are warm. Pre-exercise: dynamic stretching is preferred.

Standard 11 · INJR
Common Injuries & First Aid
9% of exam

Key Concepts

  • 01RICE method: Rest, Ice, Compression, Elevation — applied immediately post-injury
  • 02Heat stroke: hot DRY skin + altered mental status = LIFE-THREATENING — call 911, cold water immersion
  • 03Heat exhaustion: heavy sweating, normal/elevated temp, weakness — rest, fluids, cooling
  • 04Shock signs: pale, cool, clammy skin; rapid weak pulse; confusion; low blood pressure
  • 05External bleeding: direct pressure FIRST → pressure dressing → tourniquet (last resort)
  • 06Fracture immobilization: splint joint ABOVE and joint BELOW the fracture — do NOT reduce
  • 07Crutch fitting: axillary pad 2 finger-widths below armpit; hand grip = elbow 15–20° flexion
  • 08Anaphylaxis: epinephrine (EpiPen) FIRST → call 911 → transport; antihistamines are adjunct only
  • 09MRSA: methicillin-resistant staph — spreads by contact; looks like spider bite; do NOT drain in field
  • 10Sprain = LIGAMENT injury; Strain = MUSCLE or TENDON injury
  • 11Spinal injury: maintain in-line stabilization — monitor ABCs; do NOT move unless airway compromised
  • 12Contusion = bruise (no skin break); Laceration = cut; Abrasion = skin scraped; Avulsion = torn away
⚡ Exam Tip
Sprain vs Strain is guaranteed every year. Heat STROKE = hot DRY skin (sweating stops). Fracture rule = joint ABOVE and BELOW. Direct pressure is ALWAYS first for bleeding.

Standard 12 · REHB
Rehabilitation & Modalities
9% of exam

Key Concepts

  • 01Gate Control Theory: non-painful input (rubbing) can close the 'gate' on pain signals at spinal cord
  • 02Phase I: control pain/swelling, maintain CV fitness, begin early ROM
  • 03Phase II: restore full ROM, strength/power/endurance, begin proprioception
  • 04Phase III: functional/sport-specific skills, balance, return-to-sport criteria met
  • 05SAID principle: Specific Adaptation to Imposed Demands — rehab must mirror sport demands
  • 06Cryotherapy: vasoconstriction → reduced blood flow/swelling; 20–30 minutes per session
  • 07Thermotherapy: vasodilation → increased blood flow — CONTRAINDICATED in acute phase (0–72 hrs)
  • 08Ultrasound: thermal (deep heating) + non-thermal (cavitation) effects on tissue
  • 09Manual therapy: massage, cupping, IASTM/scraping (Graston technique)
  • 10Compression wrap: start at metatarsal heads (most distal), work proximally — figure-8 pattern
  • 11Overload principle: progressively increase demands to drive adaptation
  • 12Common rehab mistakes: treating symptoms not cause, ignoring contralateral side, poor posture assessment
⚡ Exam Tip
Gate Control Theory appears on almost every EOPA. Know all 3 rehab PHASES and what each focuses on. Cryotherapy = 20–30 min MAX. SAID = specific adaptation — rehabilitation must be sport-specific.

Sample Practice Questions

Q1
The Gate Control Theory of pain management proposes that:
  1. ANon-painful nerve input (like rubbing) can 'close the gate' on pain signals at the spinal cord level✓
  2. BPain signals can only be blocked by pharmaceutical analgesics
  3. CAll pain signals travel directly to the brain without modulation
  4. DGreater injury always equals proportionally greater perceived pain
Rationale: Gate Control Theory (Melzack & Wall, 1965): Large-diameter fibers (touch/vibration) can inhibit transmission of small-diameter pain fibers at the dorsal horn 'gate.' Explains why rubbing an injury helps.
Q2
The recommended duration for ice application to an acute injury is:
  1. A20–30 minutes per session✓
  2. B5–10 minutes per session
  3. C45–60 minutes per session
  4. DUntil the skin becomes completely numb and remains so
Rationale: 20–30 minutes = optimal ice time. Less = insufficient vasoconstriction. More = risk of frostbite, rebound vasodilation. Always use barrier (towel) between ice and skin.
Q3
Ultrasound as a therapeutic modality works by:
  1. AConverting sound waves to heat energy deep within tissues to increase circulation and promote healing✓
  2. BSending direct electrical current through tissue to reduce pain
  3. CUsing cold ultrasound waves to reduce swelling in acute injuries
  4. DProviding mechanical compression to the injury site
Rationale: Therapeutic ultrasound = thermal and non-thermal effects. Thermal: deep heating (increases tissue extensibility). Non-thermal: cavitation, streaming (speeds healing).
